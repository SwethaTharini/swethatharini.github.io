BELUM CAVES – A GLIMPSE INTO A DREAMY REALM

Ever remember the dreamy night from childhood after having a big day? It is full of sparkles and weird shapes, buildings and trippy locations. Feels like, for instance, we transcended to another realm in our sleep. Even though scientifically they say we mostly forget our dreams, there are these unforgettable ones we've had that we still remember till today.

Oh! How we wished it never ended and kept thinking of it again and again. Sometimes we even wished to capture it with some advanced technology so that we could take it with us to the physical world. Well at times the real world is even more bewildering than the dream.

Places that look like it jumped out of a prodigy’s complex mind. Well if you want to experience it, pack up and head to Belum Caves in Andhra Pradesh.

The Belum caves is a natural cave formation in the Kurnool district of Andhra Pradesh. It is the longest cave system in India extending upto 3229 meters and a depth of 151ft below ground. This cave system was formed as the result of erosion of the limestone deposit by the river Chitravathi.

Even though the cave system was discovered in the late 1800s the official recognition was done only after the German expedition in 1982–1983, after which it was declared as a protected zone.

Now, the Belum cave formation is a protected asset that is maintained by the Andhra Pradesh Tourism Development Corporation (APTDC).

TRANSCENDENCE:

Nestled under the agricultural lands, the entrance to the caves looks like a deep well that’s ready to take the plunge of a diver. Few steps further into the cave and you are already awe struck by the unbelievable formations of the underground world.

The long dark passages, clamouring echoes of the water, formations of stalagmite makes us feel an unbreakable sense of ataraxia.

Sliding our gaze gradually from the quartz crystal formations in the rocks, we reach the Simhadwaram (lion head shaped formation), an arch with the formation of stalactites that resembles the head of a lion.

The arch welcomes you to a spiritual trice as you come across the Kotilingalu (crore lingams) formations. The stalagmite formations occurred in thousands of numbers, each resembling the shape of a Lingam. Apart from the prominent formations you will also come across numerous shapes and patterns formed by the deposits, you can give them your own shape depending on how you perceive it.

We might think, how could this place be untouched by mankind for all these years even though it was formed millions of years ago. Caves were always seen as a tranquil place or a portal to enlightenment by spiritual people. You can see that the cave has been used by Buddhist and Jain monks centuries before as a meditation place.

Music is the vibe of the universe and underground caves do not need to always be silent and thrilling. The Saptasvarala Guha (seven swaras cave) is a marvel formed by stalactites because when you hit it with a stick it doesn’t go “tick” like a normal rock, it goes “tiinnnnngg”. At the deepest point lies the Pathalalinga, a stalagmite formation at 150ft, accompanied by an underground waterfall. The depths of the earth are awaiting your visit.

A PRECIOUS TRINKET TO YOUR TRAVEL DIARY – EMERALD LAKE

Like a sparkling precious stone to a beauty’s ears, this enticing offbeat location blinds away all the rumpus of a routine life with its magnificence. Rightfully named “Emerald”, this off-grid spot is hedged within the enthralling Western Ghats and adorned by clear emerald waters.

The fresh air from the opulent greenery and wide variety of flora and fauna is without any doubt an incredible sight. The place looks exceptionally striking during the early mornings and the evenings when the sky is kissed with peach and golden coloured clouds that look like delicious huge cotton candies floating above us.

Not to forget the lake that looks like an uncut piece of the precious emerald stone glistening in the daylight. Apart from the eye-catching scenic views there are also other fun activities like trekking, camping and bird watching activities arranged for the visitors.

REACHING: You can visit the Emerald Dam and Lake any day of the week between 6 AM to 5 PM. There is no entry fee. One can reach Emerald dam through Bus or private vehicle by reaching Ooty and taking the Avalanche road. A brief drive of 25 km will bring you to the destination. Make sure you carry enough supplies like food, water, snacks.

WHEN TO VISIT: Visiting during summer is best; monsoons and early September may have strong winds and downpours.

A TAMPERED CROWN – DHANUSHKODI

Clear blue waters gushing on all sides and the gentle waves singing melodiously for forgiveness. The distant sound of a jeep riding along a bumpy road with a dainty chatter of few people. Amidst the commingle of all these sounds is the dismal sight of a town in ruins that once flourished with a lively aura. Isolated in the southeastern tip of India, this small town has always been a visit that revokes our gratitude for being able to step out of our houses on a stormy day and not be consumed by the waters. Our search for uncharted adventure takes us to the town of Dhanushkodi which is around 20km from Rameshwaram the Island town.

HOLDING ON TO LEGACIES:

The Dhanush Kodi we see today is the remains of a town that was once a busy hub transporting goods and people to and from Sri Lanka and India. Some also say that when you stand at the very edge of the land you will be able to see the Sri Lankan islands from there. Well, it could be because the island is just 31 km from the town. It is also the place where Lord Rama built the Rama Sethu with the help of Lord Hanuman and his army to cross the sea and reach Ceylon (Sri Lanka). Some claim that the evidence of the Rama Setu can be seen as a narrow stretch of submerged rocks from an ariel view. Despite the heartbreaking catastrophe that destroyed the town in the year 1964, it continues to serve as a tourist spot helping the few fishermen families to continue their livelihood peacefully. Another surprising factor is that the town has a freshwater source from where the locals get water for daily needs. The interesting part is that the water doesnt have even a tiny hint of saltiness even though it is originating from the salty sand beach surrounded all sided by seawater. Sources claim that these water sources could be from natural aquifers. Even though the town was stricken with natural calamities it still holds on to its legacies singing the once happy and flourishing town’s praise in the tears of its ruins and beaches.

A TROPICAL SNOWFLAKE

What a beautiful composition of nature! So pure and so intricate. Soft yet invasive. Each tiny one falls with a marvellous pattern. Snow is indeed one of the most dazzling occurrences in nature. Unfortunately for some of us in the South, it is an impossible one to see. To experience the cozy cold weather and catch a snowflake we have to travel all the way up to the foothills of the Himalayas. A minimum of 15,000 Rs for the trip, food and accommodations cost additional charges. Local landscapes are concerning so you need to take a local cab. The trip needs at least a month of planning and needs at least a week time for the entire trip to be completed. All this in mind yet our heart’s longing for that tiny little snowflake to float from the heavens and fall into our hands. With desperation in mind and hundreds of references later we stumbled upon a piece of good news. There is actually a place in South India that receives snowfall during the winters.

DELIGHTFUL VENTURES:

A long road trip across the country is definitely a breathtaking experience. But the facts concerning the capital needed is always an obstruction. But why worry about it when you can visit a winter wonderland within a few hours reach. Lambasingi is a small village in Visakapattinam fondly called the Kashmir of Andra Pradesh due to its cold weather and annual snowfall in the months of November and December. It lies 1025m above sea level in the Chintapalli Ghats of Andra Pradesh. The dense deciduous forests covering the village, scenic valley views accompanied by the teeth shuttering temperatures make this an offgrid adventure destination. The temperatures do not always drop below zero but there are few times when subzero temperatures made Lambasingi a winter wonderland. Apart from these, there are also multiple adventure activities you can try out during your visit to Lambasingi. ● Camping under the stars: What can be more delightful than sleeping under the canopy of stars in a cozy tent. A warm cup of coffee and tales throughout the night before the bonfire is an irreplaceable experience. ● Trek to off-grid adventures: Grab your backpacks and head for the Kotha Palli falls located 27km from Lambasingi. ● Fill your feed with beautiful pictures visiting the Tjangi reservoir. The turquoise water with a backdrop of misty curls up the hills is an absolute eye delight. So set a page aside to fill your winter diaries with a miraculous snowfall from a hill surrounded by humid plains

ABUTMENT OF TRADITIONS – THANJAVUR

There is always this one town in every continent that outshines the rest of its peers in terms of culture. It feels like the one character in some movies that remains still when everything around it fast forwards in godspeed. One such place is Namma Thanjavur Even though it is touched up by the millennial period it still glows from within with all those unaltered authenticities. Here’s how you can release yourself a bit from the busy world and dive into the river of classical amusements.

“The vast shores of Kāvirippoompattinam are filled with flowers. Its ancient splendour with unfailing tradition resembles heaven. “ -Agananooru 280

A CLASSICAL ODYSSEY :

LASSICAL ODYSSEY : There is a reason why she is called“ South India’s Cradle of Arts” Let’s get into the actual feeling of it. Imagine the gradual starting of the Nadaswaram along with the Thavil playing Mallari in the background as we take a walk around those big halls adorned with traditional arts.

Thanjavur is the birthplace of the famous Carnatic music and also the birthplace of the Trinity of Carnatic music, St. Thyagaraja, Syama Sastri and Mutuswamy Dhikshithar. St. Thyagaraja composed numerous devotional songs in praise of Lord Rama. Even today Thyagaraja Aaradhanai is conducted at Thiruvaiyaru every year during the last week of January or the first week of February in honour of Saint Thyagaraja.

Thousands of musicians gather at Thiruvaiyaru on the banks of river Cauvery where the memorial of St. Thyagaraja is constructed. The famous Pancharatna Keertanai composed by the Saint himself is sung by the singers as an act of honour.

Another most famous classical dance that also traces its origin from Thanjavur is the Bharatanatyam. The exact time of its origin is unknown, however, it is believed to be directly descended by Lord Bhrama himself through the sage Bharata Muni. He created an entire Sanskrit text called Natya Sastra which serves as the basis for this classical dance. Bhavam, Ragam, Thaalam, Natyam are the four pillars of Bharatanatyam. Even today this classical dance form is learned and honoured by students across many countries spreading its fame across the globe.

Now, here is a fun challenge for all. Try moving your neck side to side without tilting your head or try bobbing your head front and back without moving your body. Great! If you can do it then you’re a Thalaiyatti Bommai too.

Thalaiyatti Bommai or Bobblehead doll is the signature sovereign of the region. These dolls are native to Thanjavur and their origins trace to centuries before. They are commonly used during the Navaratri festival to decorate the Golu or Show Area. They are also used as decorative items as they add a touch of perky elegance to the house. Shipped worldwide, gifted with love, passed on ingenuously we have come to the art emporium of Tanjavur. At least once in your lifetime, you would have come across a framed painting of gods or goddesses, adorned in gold borders and precious stone embedding. If you have, then kudos to you. You have witnessed the famous Thanjavur painting in all its glory. An art form that illustrates luxury, devotion and intricacy in a single frame. The Thanjavur painting is done using gold, precious stones like ruby and diamond, canvas base mostly made from muslin and a wooden plank. It can be easily identified from the bold colours and chubby looking gods and goddesses adorned with embellishments. Since gold is predominantly used in these paintings it makes a perfect gift for any good occasion. Since gold never fades you can quote your relationship with the person to be everlasting too.

Apart from these predominant art forms, there are many other classical arts having their origins in Thanjavur. Stone Sculpture, wood carving, Seppu Thirumeni ( copper statues) are the pride upheld by the artists in Thanjavur from time immemorial. Among them, Seppu Thirumeni is a very priced art form from the Sangam period as the famous Court Lady Sembian Madhevi who lived during the era of king Raja Raja Sozhan was very fond of it. Her favourite hobby was making these copper statues with intricate details depicting Gods and Goddesses.

One can experience an inner bliss just by visiting Thanjavur for once. Its music, art and colourful festivals will take you to the golden era and make you feel like a Sozha belonging to the kingdom.
